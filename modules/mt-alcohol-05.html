The general rule

s1-c1 ? callout

MCA 16-6-305(1): It is unlawful to sell, give, or otherwise provide alcoholic beverages to a person under 21 years of age, except for the narrow statutory carve-outs covered below (provision in a nonintoxicating quantity by parent/guardian, medicinal provision by physician/dentist, pharmacist with prescription, or minister/priest in connection with religious observance). The statute does NOT authorize on-premise service to under-21 patrons; the carve-out applies in private settings only.

Outcomes: K8

s1-c2 ? text

Selling to a minor is the single highest-risk error a server can make. Montana takes a progressive view: the licensee's penalty escalates with each offense in a 3-year window, and the server can be charged personally too.

Outcomes: K7

s1-c3 ? callout

What counts as an offense. Any of these triggers an entry on the licensee's record:

• A successful undercover compliance check (the Montana DOR or local police send a 19- or 20-year-old in with a real ID and you serve them)
• A documented sale to a minor that's later proven (often through an MIP arrest where the minor names the bar)
• A self-reported violation during a routine inspection

Paid promotional events with under-21 attendance count if alcohol is sold or given. The 3-year window resets on each new offense, but the prior counts stay on the record for repeat-offender escalation.

ARM 42.13.101 progressive penalty schedule

s2-c1 ? list

Per offense in any 3-year period

  • 1st offense: $250 fine
  • 2nd offense: $1,000 fine
  • 3rd offense: $1,500 fine + up to 20-day license suspension
  • 4th offense: license revocation

Outcomes: K7

s2-c2 ? callout

The licensee carries the fine — but you the server can be charged separately.

• Criminal misdemeanor under MCA 16-6-305: up to $500 fine, up to 6 months in jail, or both.
• Plus whatever the licensee fires you over.
• Plus the dram-shop civil exposure under MCA 27-1-710 if the minor went on to harm themselves or someone else.

A single sale-to-minor can produce three separate consequences for the server: criminal, employment, civil.

Outcomes: K7

s2-c3 ? callout

The placard requirement. MCA 16-3-301(6) requires every licensee to display, in a prominent place on the premises, a placard issued by the Department of Revenue stating fully the consequences for violations of Montana's alcohol-service laws. The licensee does not author the placard's wording — DOR provides it and the licensee posts it. If a routine compliance check finds the placard missing, that is a separate citation on top of any sale-to-minor case. Make sure your manager has the current DOR-issued placard visible at every register and bar station.

The narrow private-setting carve-outs (MCA 16-6-305)

s3-c1 ? text

Montana has a tightly limited set of statutory exceptions under MCA 16-6-305(1)(a). A person under 21 may be provided an alcoholic beverage only in a nonintoxicating quantity and only by: (i) the person's parent or legal guardian, (ii) a physician or dentist for medicinal purposes, (iii) a licensed pharmacist upon the prescription of a physician, or (iv) an ordained minister or priest in connection with a religious observance. The exception never permits intoxication or over-service. 'Nonintoxicating quantity' is defined in the statute as a quantity that does not produce a blood/breath/urine alcohol concentration above 0.05 or substantial/visible mental or physical impairment.

Outcomes: K8, P5

s3-c2 ? list

Conditions for the exception

  • Nonintoxicating quantity only. MCA 16-6-305(1)(c) defines this as a quantity that does NOT produce a BAC above 0.05 or substantial/visible mental or physical impairment. The exception never permits intoxication or over-service.
  • Provider must be one of the four listed in MCA 16-6-305(1)(a): parent or legal guardian; physician or dentist (medicinal); licensed pharmacist (with prescription); ordained minister or priest (religious observance). No other relationship qualifies — siblings, friends, boyfriends/girlfriends, and stepparents without legal-guardian status do NOT qualify.
  • The carve-out applies in private settings only. The plain text of MCA 16-6-305(1)(a) does not distinguish between on-premise and off-premise locations, but read together with MCA 16-3-301 (service-side rules), the practical effect is that licensed establishments cannot serve under-21 patrons even when a parent is present. A regulator reading the statute would not accept a defense of 'the parent provided it on our premises.' Refuse cleanly when the under-21 patron is at a licensed establishment.
  • Where the carve-out actually applies. Examples Montana courts have treated as falling within 16-6-305(1)(a): a parent giving a sip of wine to their child at a family dinner at home; a physician administering a small medicinal dose; an ordained minister offering communion wine; a licensed pharmacist filling a prescription. None of these involve a licensed alcohol establishment.

Outcomes: K8, P5

s3-c3 ? callout

MCA 16-6-305(1)(a) (statutory text): 'Except in the case of an alcoholic beverage provided in a nonintoxicating quantity to a person under 21 years of age by the person's parent or guardian, physician or dentist for medicinal purposes, a licensed pharmacist upon the prescription of a physician, or an ordained minister or priest in connection with a religious observance, a person may not sell or otherwise provide an alcoholic beverage to a person under 21 years of age.'

Strategies minors use, and how to spot them

s4-c1 ? list

Common tactics

  • Borrowed ID from an older sibling or friend with a similar look. Photo close but not exact — ear shape and hairline rarely match.
  • Online fake ID — often with the wrong state's holographic seal, a slightly off typeface, or a vertical layout under a 25+ DOB.
  • Proxy buyer — an adult buys for them at the door or after the sale at the curb. Watch the parking lot.
  • Distraction tactic — a group orders together, the underage member ducks behind a friend at the moment of ID-check.
  • Confidence tactic — extra-loud, extra-confident, getting friendly with the bartender as a misdirection.
  • The pass-back — a 21+ orders a round, then passes the drink to the under-21 friend at the table or in a hallway.

Outcomes: K6

s4-c2 ? text

The simplest defense is consistency. Card every customer who looks under 30, every time. The moment you start making exceptions for "they look old enough" or "they're a regular," you're carrying personal liability for the next mistake. The licensee's penalty schedule applies to the bar; the criminal misdemeanor applies to you.

s4-c4 ? callout

The 'parental permission' line at the bar. Sometimes a parent walks up with their under-21 child and says, 'I'm letting them have one beer with dinner; I'm the parent, I can authorize it.' The answer at a Montana on-premise bar is still no. MCA 16-6-305(1)(a)'s narrow exception lets a parent provide a nonintoxicating quantity in a private setting (the family kitchen, for example) — it does NOT permit an on-premise licensee to serve a minor regardless of who is consenting. The licensee's training requirement and the ARM 42.13.101 progressive penalty apply to every on-premise pour, parental consent or not. If a server pours to a minor because the parent vouched, the citation goes to the server and the licensee, the parent's consent has no legal effect. The clean refusal: 'I can't pour to anyone under 21 here, even with your okay. The state's exception is for at-home, not at-bar. Can I bring them a soda?'
