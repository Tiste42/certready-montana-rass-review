Per-se BAC limits in Montana

s1-c1 | text

Montana, like every state, has per-se BAC limits - numbers that establish a per-se alcohol or drug driving offense without separate proof of impairment. Per-se literally means "by itself": the number is the offense. Below those limits, a driver can still be charged with DUI based on observed impairment, but the per-se thresholds are the absolute lines.

s1-c3 | callout

MCA 61-8-1002 (summary): Montana's per-se DUI thresholds are codified in subsection (1) with separate subdivisions for each driver class - 0.08 alcohol concentration for non-commercial 21+ drivers, 0.04 for commercial drivers, 0.02 for drivers under 21, and 5 ng/mL Delta-9 THC (excluding inactive metabolites) for any driver. Meeting any threshold establishes per-se DUI without separate proof of impairment.

0.04 BAC at about 2 drinks in an hour

0.08 BAC at about 4 drinks in an hour

0.16 BAC (aggravated DUI line) at about 8 drinks in 2 hours Body weight matters. A 110 lb adult hits each band on roughly half the drinks. A 220 lb adult on about 30% more.

Aggravated DUI is a separate, harsher charge. It applies when:

The driver's BAC is 0.16 or higher, OR

The driver refused a chemical test AND has a prior DUI in the past 10 years Aggravated DUI carries longer mandatory jail and higher fines than a regular first-offense DUI. A bar that serves a customer past 0.16 and into aggravated-DUI territory faces stronger plaintiff arguments in any later dram-shop suit.

Implied consent (MCA 61-8-1016)

s2-c1 | text

When a person drives in Montana, they are deemed by law to have consented to a chemical test (breath, blood, or urine) on lawful arrest for DUI. The driver doesn't have to actually agree at the moment - the consent is built into the act of driving. Refusing the test triggers an automatic license suspension, separate from the DUI itself.

s2-c4 | callout

CDL holders face enhanced refusal penalties. A commercial driver who refuses a chemical test faces a 1-year disqualification for a first refusal and a lifetime disqualification for a second refusal (with possible reinstatement after 10 years under federal rules). This applies to ANY vehicle they're driving at the time of the stop, not just a commercial vehicle. A truck driver who refuses a breath test on the way home from your bar in his personal car loses his livelihood, not just his weekend. If you have CDL regulars at your bar, this is worth knowing - most of them already know the rule and treat it as the floor, not the ceiling, of their personal cut-off.

DUI penalty escalation (MCA 61-8-1007 for 1st-3rd; MCA 61-8-1008 for 4th+)

s3-c1 | list

Outcomes: K15

s3-c2 | callout

The 4th-offense lookback. Some states only count prior DUIs from a 5- or 10-year window. Montana's felony-DUI statute sets no statutory time limit, so every prior DUI conviction on a person's record still counts toward the 4th-offense felony. A first DUI at 22 and a fourth at 55 still triggers the felony. There is no "clean slate" period.

Outcomes: K15

s3-c3 | callout

Why the server should know this. When a customer at the bar is bragging about driving home or about a prior DUI, you now know what the next charge would cost them. That helps you offer a ride or call their friend with confidence: this is real money, real jail, and a felony on the fourth strike. It's also a credibility check - if a customer tells you "I've been pulled over six times and never gotten DUI" and then asks for one more, you have a clearer sense of what's actually at stake for them.

Implied consent, refusal suspensions, and the aggravated-DUI overlay

s6-c1 | text

Montana's implied-consent rule under MCA 61-8-1016 is one of the strongest tools on the law-enforcement side of the DUI framework. By operating a motor vehicle on a Montana road, a driver is deemed to have consented in advance to a blood, breath, or oral-fluid test if a peace officer has probable cause to believe the driver is impaired. Refusing the test triggers an administrative license suspension under MCA 61-8-1032, completely separate from any criminal DUI conviction. For a first refusal, the suspension is 6 months with no possibility of a restricted probationary license. For a second-or-subsequent refusal within 5 years, the suspension is 1 year, again with no restricted-license option. Every refused implied-consent test also carries a $300 administrative fee payable to the department before the license can be reinstated. Commercial driver's license holders carry an even sharper consequence: a first refusal triggers a 1-year CDL disqualification under federal rules; a subsequent refusal triggers lifetime CDL disqualification. The server-side takeaway is straightforward - a customer you over-serve who then refuses a breath test the same night is looking at the criminal DUI charge AND a 6-month or 1-year administrative suspension AND a $300 fee, none of which depend on the prosecutor proving impairment in court.

Outcomes: K13

s6-c2 | callout

**Aggravated DUI at 0.16

the higher-tier threshold under MCA 61-8-1001. Montana defines 'aggravated driving under the influence' to include a per-se threshold of 0.16 BAC** (twice the standard 0.08 per-se DUI level under MCA 61-8-1002). A driver charged at or above 0.16 faces enhanced penalties at every offense level: longer minimum jail terms, higher fines, longer ignition-interlock requirements, and (for repeat offenders) a faster path to the 4th-offense felony framework under MCA 61-8-1008. The aggravated-DUI threshold is also a useful number to know on the service side: a 180-lb adult male reaches 0.16 BAC after roughly 8 standard drinks in an hour on an empty stomach; a 130-lb adult female reaches it after roughly 6. When you've watched a customer order their fifth or sixth round in a short window, the question on the table is not 'will they fail a breath test' but 'will they fail it at the aggravated level.' That's the math behind 'cut them off two rounds earlier than feels comfortable.' The 0.16 threshold also matters because it shifts the criminal-procedure picture: an aggravated DUI in Montana is one of the small set of misdemeanors where the prosecution often does not negotiate the charge down, and the mandatory minimum jail term on a first aggravated conviction is materially longer than a standard-tier first DUI. From the licensee's perspective, an aggravated-DUI defendant who was served at your bar is also the kind of defendant whose attorney will work backward from the breath-test result to the last bartender to pour

meaning the dram-shop notice under MCA 27-1-710(8) is more likely to arrive within the 180-day window when the underlying criminal case is aggravated than when it is a standard-tier first offense. For Montana servers, that converts directly into a service rule: when the table's drink count and timeline are tracking toward 0.16, the cost-benefit of cutting off two rounds early is overwhelmingly in favor of the cut.
