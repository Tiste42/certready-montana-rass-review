Module 2: Alcohol and the Body

BAC, absorption, standard drinks, and how Montana defines an alcoholic beverage

What counts as alcohol in Montana

s1-c1 | text

Montana defines an alcoholic beverage as any beverage with more than 0.5% alcohol by volume (MCA 16-1-106(4)). That includes beer, wine, spirits, cider, hard seltzer, ready-to-drink cocktails, and pre-mixed canned drinks. Anything stronger than non-alcoholic beer falls under Montana's alcohol laws and triggers every rule in this course: ID checks, refusal-of-service obligations, dram shop liability under MCA 27-1-710, and the hours-of-service rules in MCA 16-3-304.

Outcomes: K3

s1-c2 | callout

MCA 16-1-106(4) (verbatim): "'Alcoholic beverage' means a compound produced and sold for human consumption as a drink that contains more than 0.5% of alcohol by volume."

Outcomes: K3

s1-c3 | callout

The 0.5% line at the bar. A few common items sit close to the threshold. Most kombucha is 0.5-2.5% ABV; the high end is alcohol under Montana law. "Non-alcoholic" beer is legally capped at 0.5% ABV, so it falls outside the rules, but a so-called "NA" craft option that prints 0.6% is in. Hard kombucha (3-9%) is fully alcoholic. When a customer says "it's just a kombucha," check the label.

Standard drinks: the math behind every pour

s2-c1 | text

Servers and bartenders need to think in standard drink equivalents because that is the unit alcohol affects the body in. The CDC and NIAAA define one standard drink as approximately 14 grams (0.6 fluid ounces) of pure ethanol. That works out to: 12 oz of beer at 5% ABV, 5 oz of wine at 12% ABV, or 1.5 oz of distilled spirits at 40% ABV (80 proof). All three put the same amount of alcohol into the bloodstream.

Outcomes: K3, P1

s2-c3 | callout

The pitfall. Customers count drinks. The body counts ounces of pure alcohol. A customer who tells you "I've only had two" but those two were 16 oz strong craft beers at 9% ABV has had the equivalent of almost 5 standard drinks. The lesson: in your head, translate every order to standard drinks before you decide whether to pour the next one.

Outcomes: K3, P1

How alcohol moves through the body

s3-c1 | text

Alcohol is absorbed mostly through the small intestine. About 20% absorbs through the stomach lining; the rest moves into the small intestine and crosses into the bloodstream. Once in the blood it reaches every organ within minutes-brain, heart, liver, kidneys. The brain is the one that matters for service decisions: alcohol depresses central nervous system function, which is why coordination, judgment, and reaction time deteriorate before slurred speech and stumbling become visible.

Outcomes: K2

s3-c2 | callout

The 20-40 minute window. Alcohol takes 20 to 40 minutes to fully absorb after a drink. So a customer who pounded three shots at 9:00 PM may not show full impairment until 9:30 PM, when their BAC is still climbing. The most dangerous moments often come AFTER you've stopped serving someone-the alcohol they already drank is still hitting them. This is why "one for the road" is the worst possible service decision: you are pushing their final BAC even higher when it's already rising on its own.

What this means for service decisions

s6-c1 | text

All of this physiology rolls up into one practical rule: you cannot serve based on drink count alone. A 240 lb regular drinker who has had four beers in two hours may be fine. A 110 lb first-timer who has had two cocktails on an empty stomach may be visibly intoxicated. You watch the person, not the receipt.

Outcomes: K2

s6-c2 | text

In practice, the working tools for monitoring a customer are: count standard drinks (not menu items), track time on premises, watch for behavioral cues from the BAC bands above, and pay attention to context-has this person eaten, are they on medications, did they show up already affected? When in doubt, slow the service. The goal is not always to refuse the next drink; the goal is to keep the customer at a BAC that is safe for them and legal for you to serve.

Outcomes: K2

s6-c3 | callout

The slowing-service playbook. These are not refusals-they are how seasoned bartenders keep customers and licenses safe at the same time:

Suggest a water back with the next drink.

Suggest food: "the kitchen still has wings, want me to grab you an order?"

Slow the pour: take a longer route back to the bar, restock, take 5 extra minutes between rounds.

Switch to lower-ABV options: "how about a Coors Banquet? Great session beer."

Engage in conversation: a chatty bartender is a slower-pour bartender.

If the table is having a serious moment, refill water glasses and suggest the appetizer menu.

Outcomes: K2

s6-c4 | text

Why slow service is also better business. Beyond the legal floor, the licensee's bottom line depends on customers coming back. A patron who is over-served on Tuesday and wakes up sick on Wednesday is unlikely to return; a patron who was served thoughtfully and left at a manageable BAC is the regular who walks back in next Friday. National hospitality data has consistently shown that bars with strong service-pacing cultures retain customers longer and generate higher per-visit revenue from food and non-alcohol items than bars that simply push drinks. The slowing-service playbook in the previous chunk is not a workaround for a refusal; it is a posture that lengthens the average visit, increases food attach, and reduces the catastrophic-event risk that ends licensee careers. Montana DOR investigators interviewed in 2024 industry roundtables consistently identify 'a strong service-pacing culture' as the single best predictor of a clean licensee record over a 5-year horizon, well ahead of any single training feature or technology investment. The pattern shows up in the casualty data too: industry-aggregated claim records suggest that bars with documented service-pacing policies and incident-log discipline file dram-shop claims at roughly one-third the rate of comparable bars without those practices. The licensee who treats pacing as a culture (not a checklist item) buys themselves margin in three ways at once - better customer retention, lower insurance loss ratios, and a measurably reduced probability of a catastrophic over-service event ever reaching the courthouse.

Module 3: Recognizing Intoxication and Refusing Service

Montana's 'actually, apparently, or obviously intoxicated' standard and how to refuse cleanly

The Montana legal standard

s1-c1 | text

Montana law uses TWO parallel tests for over-service. MCA 16-3-301(4)(b) prohibits selling alcohol to a person who is 'actually, apparently, or obviously intoxicated'

the three-prong phrase. 'Actually' covers a person who really is impaired

even if the signs are subtle. 'Apparently' covers a person who looks impaired to a reasonable observer; this is the standard that applies most often at the bar. 'Obviously' covers the loud, falling-down case that anyone could see from across the room. MCA 16-6-304 is a separate, broader prohibition: it bars selling, serving, or giving an alcoholic beverage to a person who is 'apparently under the influence of alcohol' (a single-prong test). The two statutes work together: refuse if either standard is met.

Outcomes: K4

s1-c2 | callout

MCA 16-3-301(4)(b) (paraphrase, statutory phrase verbatim): Montana law prohibits selling or giving alcoholic beverages to "any person actually, apparently, or obviously intoxicated" - the bracketed three-prong phrase is the statute's exact operative language.

Outcomes: K4

s1-c3 | text

Practical translation. If you have any reasonable belief the person is impaired, the law treats that as enough. You do not need a breathalyzer. You do not need a confession. The 'apparent' test is your professional judgment based on what the person is doing in front of you. The reason Montana wrote the standard this way is to give servers cover when they make the call: a court will not require you to prove a customer's exact BAC before refusing a pour. The standard is what a reasonable server would conclude.

Outcomes: K4

s1-c4 | callout

Why the dual-statute approach matters. MCA 16-3-301(4)(b) gives the three-prong service-side test ('actually, apparently, or obviously intoxicated'). MCA 16-6-304 adds a separate, broader prohibition using a single-prong test ('apparently under the influence of alcohol'). The three-prong test asks a server to refuse not only on visible cues but also on what the server actually knows about the customer (e.g., the third double in 20 minutes); the single-prong test in 16-6-304 catches anyone who appears under the influence to a reasonable observer. Tolerance does not change either standard. *Note: Montana's civil dram-shop trigger under MCA 27-1-710 uses yet a third statutory phrase

'visibly intoxicated' plus 'knew or should have known'

covered in Module 6.*

The customer who arrives already drunk

s3-c1 | text

Not every over-service problem starts at your bar. A customer who walked in from another establishment, a wedding reception, or a private party may already be in the 0.13-0.20 band before they order their first drink from you. Montana law (MCA 16-3-301(4)(b)) does not require you to know they drank elsewhere. It just requires you not to serve them while they meet the actual / apparent / obvious standard. Showing up impaired is on them; pouring the next drink is on you.

Outcomes: K4, P1

s3-c2 | callout

The bar-hopping reality. A bachelor party, a sports-team celebration, or a wedding reception will often arrive at your bar after 60-120 minutes of drinking elsewhere. Posture-check the group as they walk in: are anyone's eyes already glassy, anyone unsteady, anyone overly loud? The first 90 seconds at the door are the cleanest moment to refuse. Once you've poured a round, refusing the second is harder. Train your hostess and door staff to flag the group to the bartender before drinks land.

The 30-second rule. When you decide to intervene, do it within 30 seconds of the trigger moment. Behavioral cues, eye signs, and the BAC-band timeline a Montana server should know cold

s9-c1 | text

The BAC bands and what they look like at the bar. A 0.02-0.03 BAC band is the 'social loosening' band

slightly relaxed conversation, a small drop in inhibition, no observable motor change. A 0.04-0.06 band is the 'feeling it' band

louder, friendlier, slight reduction in fine motor control (the pour-cup-to-mouth motion gets a little less precise). A 0.07-0.10 band is the 'visible impairment' band

slurred speech is now apparent to a sober observer, balance becomes noticeably affected, judgment is measurably worse. Above 0.10 the cluster gets unmistakable: pronounced slurring, swaying, glassy and bloodshot eyes, difficulty completing basic motor tasks like counting cash or unlocking a phone screen. Montana's apparent-intoxication standard under MCA 16-3-301(4)(b) and MCA 16-6-304 is calibrated to the 0.07-and-above bands

a server who can describe two or three cues from those bands has met the legal standard for refusal. the cluster that's hard for a customer to hide. Behavioral cues like slurring and swaying are what most servers learn to spot first, but eye signs are the cluster most resistant to a practiced 'I'm fine' performance. Three to watch for at the bar: (1) horizontal gaze nystagmus**

The 180-day notice window under MCA 27-1-710(8) - what the bar will actually see. A dram-shop case in Montana starts with a certified-mail notice to the licensee. The notice must arrive within 180 days of the sale or service alleged to have caused the injury. The notice typically lists the date of service, the licensee's name, a brief factual description, and the law firm representing the injured party. From the bar's perspective, the 180-day window is the practical hinge: if surveillance video is on a 90-day overwrite cycle (standard for most retail systems), the footage may already be gone by the time the notice lands. The incident log, the training records, the receipts showing the customer's tab, and the staff-list-for-that-shift are the artifacts most likely to survive the gap. The single best preservation move a bar can make is to keep video archived for at least 200 days for any night where any incident report was filed, and to keep incident logs for the full 5-year statute window. The cost of additional video storage is trivial compared to the cost of going into a dram-shop case with no footage of the customer at the bar.

s6-c4 | callout

The MCA 27-1-710(10) damage caps are not unlimited protection. Implied consent, refusal suspensions, and the aggravated-DUI overlay

s6-c1 | text

Montana's implied-consent rule under MCA 61-8-1016 is one of the strongest tools on the law-enforcement side of the DUI framework. By operating a motor vehicle on a Montana road, a driver is deemed to have consented in advance to a blood, breath, or oral-fluid test if a peace officer has probable cause to believe the driver is impaired. Refusing the test triggers an administrative license suspension under MCA 61-8-1032, completely separate from any criminal DUI conviction. For a first refusal, the suspension is 6 months with no possibility of a restricted probationary license. For a second-or-subsequent refusal within 5 years, the suspension is 1 year, again with no restricted-license option. Every refused implied-consent test also carries a $300 administrative fee payable to the department before the license can be reinstated. Commercial driver's license holders carry an even sharper consequence: a first refusal triggers a 1-year CDL disqualification under federal rules; a subsequent refusal triggers lifetime CDL disqualification. The server-side takeaway is straightforward - a customer you over-serve who then refuses a breath test the same night is looking at the criminal DUI charge AND a 6-month or 1-year administrative suspension AND a $300 fee, none of which depend on the prosecutor proving impairment in court.

the higher-tier threshold under MCA 61-8-1001.
